At the outset of the study we decided on a definition of ‘television’ and we took our inspiration from an Oxford Dictionaries entry that is as relevant today as it was 90 years ago:
television, noun - a system for converting visual images (with sound) into electrical signals, signals, transmitting them by radio or other means, and displaying them electronically on a screen.
In other words television is not an object (unlike the TV set), television is a creative medium that can be transmitted and dis- played in a variety of forms, as such we informed our respondents that they should consider television to be all long form broadcast quality video irrespective of how or where consumed.

If some had predicted that the arrival of digital and all its forms of entertainment and distractions would overwhelm television, then in fact the opposite happened, digital has come along and liberated television from the box that it was previously held in.

Television has been unleashed, colonising digital screens

BEYOND...ENTERTAINMENT

Why has television been so resilient in the face of this unprecedented shift in the media landscape, where others have floundered?

Above all it comes down to the deeply emotional and human experience of television. It uniquely has the power to move us and in doing so creates powerful bonds.


86% of us say television prompts sentiment – making us cry, dream or think; 71% say that television makes them excited, upset or truly elated; 60% claim that television helps them to escape their worries


“I became hooked on the show ‘Six Feet Under’. The series finale ended...and I was in tears for about 30 minutes after...the way it was made, made me think of the concept of death in a whole new way”

And it’s not just an emotional relationship that we have with television it’s also an intellectual one. Television acts as educator, influence and trusted advisor


78% say television helps them learn about the world around them and 70% say television shapes and influences their opinion


“(Television provides) unexpected questions, situations, perspectives and thoughts that can come about as it makes you think differently and expands your horizons”

Television made the world a smaller place long before the internet and continues to be the pre-eminent place to get our trusted information from. The advent of ‘multi-screening’ has facilitated the curiosity to learn more ignited by television


67% will use a second screen whilst watching the TV set, 47% stating that they enjoy the freedom to discover more about what they’re watching


“When my boyfriend was watching ‘Narcos’, and I thought I might get into that show...I Googled about the character that it’s about...and found the story really interesting”

And it is the strength of the emotional and intellectual connection that makes us want to share our television thoughts and experiences with others


during or after television shows 64% of us talk or message friends and family, 52% of us go online to discover more and 42% share, tweet or blog online


“Talking with friends about a TV Show? OMG - Daily at work!! During the programmes texting too!! During the 3 weeks of “I’m a Celeb” there was ongoing daily discussion about the behaviour and events of the show”

Television was the original social network and it continues to set the cultural agenda, the digital sphere has allowed us to express our emotional and intellectual relationship with television to an ever wider audience in ever increasing amounts.

BEYOND...METRICS

[Intro]

The emotional bond between television and audiences translates into a powerful advertising environment that similarly can’t be replicated by other media


82% of respondents felt that television advertising evoked an emotion print/radio/OOH (x1.7) – which translated to 60% stating that television advertising provoked a call to action, compared to just 24% compared to 47% for ads on websites (x1.7) and 46% ads in for websites.


“Online advertising is almost always done badly. Popups, video ads, etc make me annoyed, and I have built up a big filter. Youtube ads I just click away without ever noticing, if a webpage has popup ads I close the browser and never visit the site again”

One of the fastest growing media is short-form online video with the likes of Youtube and Facebook claiming billions of views a day. It is also one of the fastest growing advertising media, so we asked our respondents a number of questions about how viewing an ad in long form television compared to short form. The first thing they said is that they don’t notice online video advertising


63% of respondents claimed to pay attention to advertising during/around live television compared to 39% for online video


“I just wait for ‘Skip now’. When I’m going to YouTube I want to watch something straight away. I know it’s got to be there or it’s not going to make them money, but as soon as ‘Skip now’ comes on, that’s it”

Short form video is transient in nature and far less emotionally engaging than long form television and this transposes on the way advertising is consumed even when it’s the same ad. We asked respondents “if you were to see the same ad in two different places: once as part of a commercial break during a TV show, and once before watching a short video clip online. Would you feel differently about the ad, based on the context in which you saw it?” and the results were overwhelming:


41% felt that the ad would be trustworthy in television compared to 14% in short form, and 44% felt that the advertising would be engaging compared to 16% in short form. Conversely 38% felt that an ad around short form video is distracting and annoying compared to just 17% for television.


“having to sit through a 30sec advert to watch a short video clip is not acceptable, so I close the video”

The emotional bond does not exist for short form video, and its transient nature makes this an environment where permission to enter is more limited. In contrast television sells a unique currency, one which delivers a quality of attention and identification with plots, characters and real life stories that people want to know about or be part of


66% of respondents agreed that “television sells unique experiences and cultural currency that extends well beyond the live broadcast” with 69% saying television ignited conversations and therefore 50% agreeing that television advertising can ignite conversations.


“TV is an integral part of my life, it's where my family gathers and it helps start conversations”

Television truly encapsulates the human experience – this is why television is about more than just traffic, views or clicks. The emotive allure of television relates to all of us in different ways.

BEYOND...DEMOGRAPHICS

Television relates to us all in different ways, and our relationship is dictated by a number of behavioural and emotional relationships. The way we consume and use television cannot be summarised by how old we are or whether we or male and female. We reject the notion that 18-24 year olds consume television one way and over 50’s another, the landscape is far more nuanced.

Therefore we created a number of statistically robust personas to give a perspective on how television is really consumed in 2016


Companions (22%) Television has always been an important part of their life; it entertains them, expands their knowledge, and is the best way for them to check-in on world events. They prefer to watch on a TV set and usually live programmes. For them television is a constant reliable companion and if they’ve watched something impactful it can stay with them for a long time, it can also give them something to talk (or laugh) about with their friends.


Balancers (10%) Television has to fit around them and their busy life, and they really rely on catch-up and on-demand services. Television is their personal haven, it helps them to escape and unwind away from their busy and sometimes stressful week. Watching television can be with their family, but often it is golden ‘me-time’ for them when they can watch what they want.

Therefore we created a number of statistically robust personas to give a perspective on how television is really consumed in 2016


Lovers (19%) Television is everywhere – it is the first thing they put on when they wake up and the last thing to help them switch off at night, and new technology has given them more television than ever. They love talking to their friends about television either on Facebook or at work the next day. Television is their playground – it is best when it brings all the family together, tucking into the latest must-watch or indulging in a boxset or a movie. It’s not uncommon to see them reaching for a tissue, sitting on the edge of their seat, or shouting at the screen.


Projectors (6%) Television is digitally enabled – it is programmes that can be found anywhere and everywhere and on any of their devices. They try to keep on top of new shows and are generally the first of their friends to start watching new series. As well as watching live, they watch a lot of programs on-demand or through streaming services like Netflix. They like to have their smartphone to-hand so they can look up something about a character or a storyline, Tweet or post on Facebook.

Therefore we created a number of statistically robust personas to give a perspective on how television is really consumed in 2016


Selectives (21%) Television is part of their toolkit – it sits alongside many other forms of media such as newspapers, magazines and the internet as something they’ll pick up as and when they need it. They would say they’re quite discerning in what they choose to watch, and they don’t just put on any old rubbish. They often explore online after they’ve watched something to find out more, television can be an open window to the world, an opportunity to learn.


Sceptics (22%) Television is an acquaintance – it doesn’t play a huge role in their life. They wouldn’t want to pay extra for channels they won’t really watch and tend to just stick to free-to-air, live television when they are watching. They don’t know if they’ll need a television in their house in the future or whether it will have a prominent place – they just don’t watch it enough. Sceptics say television is a time killer alongside the internet – they don’t get hugely emotional about these types of media.

Across the personas the average age is similar (slightly younger for Projectors), the main differentials are in their relationship with technology and their attitude towards television. When looking at television advertising through these personas we get some fascinating results, taking the 30second ad in long form vs. short form environments


Long form television x times more powerful than short form at being trustworthy – Companions x5.6; Balancers x2.0; Lovers x8.8, Projectors x1.2; Selectives x2.7; Sceptics x1.6

Everyone has a connection with television in some form or another, how that connection differs is down to your emotional and intellectual need states, and not how old you are.

BEYOND...2016

Television in 2016 and beyond is like television used to be, but more so. More choice, more access, more flexibility and more power to engage, inspire, excite & delight like no other media. In an ever evolving media landscape the position of television as #1 for advertising effectiveness remains unchallenged and that’s thanks to the continued unique place it occupies in the hearts and minds of consumers.
